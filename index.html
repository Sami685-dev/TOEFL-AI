PASSAGE

The Science of Sleep


Sleep is a vital biological process that affects nearly every system in the body. During sleep, the brain consolidates memories, repairs tissues, releases growth hormones, and processes emotional experiences. Scientists have identified two primary types of sleep: Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep and Non-Rapid Eye Movement (NREM) sleep.


NREM sleep comprises approximately 75–80% of total sleep time and is divided into three stages. The first stage is a light transitional phase lasting just a few minutes. The second stage is characterized by sleep spindles — bursts of neural activity that are thought to play a role in memory consolidation. The third stage, known as slow-wave sleep (SWS), is the deepest and most restorative phase, during which blood pressure drops, breathing slows, and the body performs most of its physical repair work.


REM sleep, which accounts for the remaining 20–25% of the sleep cycle, is when most dreaming occurs. The brain during REM is nearly as active as it is when awake. Researchers believe REM sleep is crucial for emotional regulation and creative problem-solving. A hallmark feature of REM is muscle atonia — temporary paralysis of voluntary muscles — which prevents individuals from physically acting out their dreams.


Sleep deprivation has serious consequences. Even one night of insufficient sleep can impair cognitive function, reduce immune response, and increase the risk of accidents. Chronic sleep deprivation is linked to a higher incidence of obesity, cardiovascular disease, and mental health disorders such as depression and anxiety. The recommended amount of sleep for adults is 7–9 hours per night, while teenagers require 8–10 hours.


Despite widespread awareness of its importance, many people worldwide do not get adequate sleep due to work demands, social habits, screen exposure, and sleep disorders such as insomnia or sleep apnea. Healthcare professionals increasingly emphasize sleep hygiene — a set of behavioral and environmental practices — as a foundational pillar of overall health.

📄 TRANSCRIPT

Professor Chen begins the lecture:


"Good morning, everyone. Today we're going to discuss a topic that's become increasingly urgent in modern environmental science: the phenomenon of ocean acidification and its cascading effects on marine ecosystems.


So, what exactly is ocean acidification? When carbon dioxide — CO₂ — is released into the atmosphere, a significant portion of it — roughly 25 to 30 percent — is absorbed by the world's oceans. When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it reacts with water molecules to form carbonic acid. This, in turn, dissociates and releases hydrogen ions, which lowers the ocean's pH. Since the Industrial Revolution, the average ocean pH has dropped from approximately 8.2 to around 8.1 — that's a 26% increase in acidity, because pH is measured on a logarithmic scale.


Now, some of you might think, 'That doesn't sound like much.' But even small pH changes have dramatic biological consequences. Marine organisms that build shells or skeletons from calcium carbonate — like oysters, clams, sea urchins, and corals — are particularly vulnerable. In more acidic water, carbonate ions become less available, making it harder and more energetically costly for these animals to build and maintain their structures. In severe cases, existing shells can actually dissolve.


Coral reefs are of special concern. They cover less than 1% of the ocean floor, yet they support approximately 25% of all marine species. As reefs bleach and dissolve due to combined heat stress and acidification, entire food webs collapse. Fish populations that depend on reef habitats decline, which in turn affects the roughly one billion people worldwide who rely on seafood as their primary protein source.


Researchers are exploring several mitigation strategies. One approach is adding alkaline materials like crushed limestone to the ocean — a process called ocean liming — to locally neutralize acidity. Another promising avenue is expanding seagrass and kelp forests, which absorb CO₂ through photosynthesis and can create localized refuges with higher pH levels.


However, most scientists agree that the most effective long-term solution is reducing CO₂ emissions at the source. Without substantial cuts in global emissions, the ocean will continue to acidify at a rate that outpaces the adaptive capacity of most marine organisms."
