Section 1 — Reading Comprehension

Passage

The Science of Sleep


Sleep is a vital biological process that affects nearly every system in the body. During sleep, the brain consolidates memories, repairs tissues, releases growth hormones, and processes emotional experiences. Scientists have identified two primary types of sleep: Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep and Non-Rapid Eye Movement (NREM) sleep.


NREM sleep comprises approximately 75–80% of total sleep time and is divided into three stages. The first stage is a light transitional phase lasting just a few minutes. The second stage is characterized by sleep spindles — bursts of neural activity that are thought to play a role in memory consolidation. The third stage, known as slow-wave sleep (SWS), is the deepest and most restorative phase, during which blood pressure drops, breathing slows, and the body performs most of its physical repair work.


REM sleep, which accounts for the remaining 20–25% of the sleep cycle, is when most dreaming occurs. The brain during REM is nearly as active as it is when awake. Researchers believe REM sleep is crucial for emotional regulation and creative problem-solving. A hallmark feature of REM is muscle atonia — temporary paralysis of voluntary muscles — which prevents individuals from physically acting out their dreams.


Sleep deprivation has serious consequences. Even one night of insufficient sleep can impair cognitive function, reduce immune response, and increase the risk of accidents. Chronic sleep deprivation is linked to a higher incidence of obesity, cardiovascular disease, and mental health disorders such as depression and anxiety. The recommended amount of sleep for adults is 7–9 hours per night, while teenagers require 8–10 hours.


Despite widespread awareness of its importance, many people worldwide do not get adequate sleep due to work demands, social habits, screen exposure, and sleep disorders such as insomnia or sleep apnea. Healthcare professionals increasingly emphasize sleep hygiene — a set of behavioral and environmental practices — as a foundational pillar of overall health.
